Remarkably Bright Creatures Review: Sally Field’s Tender Netflix Drama (2026)

Updated: May 8, 2026 10 min read

TL;DR

Netflix dropped Remarkably Bright Creatures on May 8, 2026 — an adaptation of Shelby Van Pelt’s bestselling novel about a grieving aquarium janitor and a wise Pacific octopus. Sally Field gets her best leading vehicle in a decade, with Lewis Pullman in support. Critics: 74% Rotten Tomatoes, 57 Metacritic. Some books seem unfilmable not because they are too complex but because so much of what makes them special lives in their voice. Shelby Van Pelt’s 2022 debut novel Remarkably Bright Creatures sold roughly two million copies on the strength of one such voice — a giant Pacific octopus named Marcellus, narrating his life in an aquarium tank with sly omniscience. Netflix’s adaptation, which premiered on May 8, 2026, was always going to live or die on whether it could honor that voice without making it twee. The verdict: it gets close enough.

The Setup

Tova Sullivan, played by Sally Field, is a widow in her seventies who took a night-shift cleaning job at the Sowell Bay Aquarium after losing her son to drowning thirty years earlier. The aquarium is quiet at night. The exhibits hum. And Marcellus, a giant Pacific octopus with an unusual gift for observation, takes an interest in her. Lewis Pullman’s Cameron, a drifting young man with his own unspoken history, eventually enters the picture.

The novel weaves these three perspectives — Tova, Cameron, and Marcellus himself — into a story about loneliness, grief, and the quiet people who notice each other. The film does the same, with one big handicap: Marcellus’s narration, the book’s most beloved feature, is dramatically reduced.

Sally Field Is the Whole Movie

Even reviewers who were lukewarm on the film were unanimous on this point. Sally Field gives one of her most concentrated performances in years. Tova is the kind of grief-shaped person who has been operating at half-light for so long that she has forgotten there is another setting — and Field finds that exact register without a single actorly underline. Variety compared it to her work in Hello, My Name Is Doris, calling it her most snugly-tailored leading role in a decade. The Hollywood Reporter noted that the rest of the film feels lighter every time Field is offscreen.

If you came to Netflix this week looking for a single reason to watch, that is it.

Lewis Pullman’s Cameron

Pullman is having a quietly excellent few years (Top Gun: Maverick, Salem’s Lot, Outer Range), and his Cameron continues a streak of low-key, internal performances. He plays Cameron as a young man who has been rounded down by life one too many times — not bitter, just tired in a way that should not be possible at his age. The chemistry with Field is the film’s emotional engine.

Where the Adaptation Stumbles

The book’s charm came from Marcellus’s wry narration — a voice that is part nature documentary, part Edith Wharton, part bored genius watching humans bumble. The film keeps small slivers of that voice but mostly externalizes Marcellus’s perspective into visual storytelling. That is a defensible choice. It is also a flattening one. Without the narration, Marcellus becomes mostly a beautifully animated CGI character rather than a co-lead.

The screenplay is the second sticking point. Critics have widely flagged the film’s tendency to telegraph its emotional reveals, particularly the central plot connection between the three characters. The book lets you piece it together; the film holds the reveal a beat too long, then lands on it with a thud. None of this is fatal. The performances carry it. But the script does not always trust the audience.

Should You Watch It?

Watch it if:

  • You loved The Father, Marcel the Shell with Shoes On, or A Man Called Otto
  • You want a low-stakes, emotionally generous Sunday-afternoon Netflix watch
  • You read the book and want to see Field’s take on Tova
  • You like films about grief that do not wallow in it

Skip it if:

  • You are sensitive to overtly sentimental storytelling
  • You want the book’s octopus narration intact (you will be disappointed)
  • You need plot momentum — this is a slow film by design

The Bigger Picture: Netflix and the Tearjerker

Netflix has been quietly building a strong slate of older-skewing prestige weepies in the last two years — The Tutor, Hannah, the surprise hit The Lost Daughters — and Remarkably Bright Creatures fits that lineage cleanly. These films do not chase the algorithmic sweet spot of true-crime or YA romance, but they consistently land top-10 placements with a viewer demographic Netflix knows it has been underserving.

Not the home run the book deserved, but a sincere, well-acted Netflix Sunday film with a Sally Field performance that is worth two hours by itself. 3.5 / 5.
