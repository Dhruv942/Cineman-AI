Twenty years on, The Devil Wears Prada has aged into the rare comedy that gets better, not worse. Meryl Streep's Miranda Priestly is genuinely terrifying. Anne Hathaway's Andy is more sympathetic than people remembered. The film's question — how much of yourself you should give to a job — lands harder in 2026 than it did in 2006.

If you have just rewatched it (or you are anticipating Devil Wears Prada 2 and want a warm-up), here are 15 films that share at least one of its core ingredients.

Pick by What You Loved About Devil Wears Prada

If you loved Miranda Priestly

The mentor-tyrant dynamic is the engine of Devil Wears Prada. Films that nail it: Phantom Thread (Day-Lewis as the impossible designer), Bombshell (Roger Ailes as the original toxic boss), The Proposal (Bullock playing the same archetype lighter), Morning Glory (Harrison Ford as the impossible morning-show anchor). These are the picks if Streep was your favorite part of the film.

If you loved Andy's transformation

The career-ascendance arc is the other emotional center. Working Girl is the canonical example — an ambitious assistant outmaneuvers her boss in 1980s Manhattan. The Intern flips it: Hathaway is now the boss, with De Niro as the unlikely mentor. 13 Going on 30 is the magical-realist version of the same wish-fulfillment.

If you loved the fashion world

Cruella, Phantom Thread, House of Gucci, and Coco Before Chanel are the fashion-as-stakes picks. Cruella is the closest in spectacle. Phantom Thread is the most artful and the least like Devil Wears Prada in tone — it is a quiet, unsettling chamber piece. House of Gucci is the most operatic.

If you loved the New York comedy of it

Bridget Jones's Diary (London, but same DNA), How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days, 27 Dresses, Set It Up, and Morning Glory all live in the same ambitious-young-woman-in-the-city register. Set It Up is the underrated pick of the bunch — sharper writing than its trailer suggested.

Why Devil Wears Prada Endures

The film has roughly 2.5x the streaming watch hours per month it had in 2010. That is not normal for a 20-year-old comedy. Two reasons:

First, Streep's performance is one of the cleanest examples of comedic restraint in the modern era. She never raises her voice. The script gives her almost no obvious comic lines. The whole performance is glares, sighs, and the famous cerulean-blue sweater monologue.

Second, the film's central question — "how much of your life is the job worth" — resonates differently with each generation that finds it. Millennials watched it as a workplace comedy. Gen Z watches it as a horror movie about hustle culture. Both readings are correct.
