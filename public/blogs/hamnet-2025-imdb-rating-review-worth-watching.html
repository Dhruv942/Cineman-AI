Hamnet is the rare prestige drama that arrived with critical hype and mostly delivered. Adapted from Maggie O'Farrell's 2020 novel about William Shakespeare's wife Agnes (commonly known as Anne Hathaway) and the death of their 11-year-old son, the film positions itself less as a Shakespeare biopic and more as a study of grief.

Director Chloé Zhao (Nomadland, The Eternals) brings the same patient, naturalistic camera that defined her best work. Paul Mescal plays Shakespeare; Jessie Buckley plays Agnes; the film is largely Buckley's, and her Best Actress Oscar acknowledged that.

What Hamnet Is Actually About

Despite the William Shakespeare association, Hamnet is not really about Shakespeare. It is about Agnes, his wife — a woman with deep folk knowledge of plants, healing, and the natural world — and the way she navigates the death of her son.

Hamnet, the boy, dies of plague at age 11. The historical record on him is thin. What we know is that four years later, William wrote a play called Hamlet (an interchangeable spelling at the time) about a son who cannot let his father go. The film treats this as the great unspoken fact of Shakespeare's career — the play that gave him his canonical reputation began as a private exorcism.

The film is structured around three timelines: Agnes and William's courtship, Hamnet's brief life and death, and the writing of Hamlet four years later. Zhao cuts between them with patience. Plot, in the conventional sense, is not the engine. Grief is.

Why It Works

Jessie Buckley

Buckley's Oscar was earned. Her Agnes is intuitive, weather-beaten, and capable of conveying enormous interior weight with stillness. The film's most devastating sequence — Agnes preparing Hamnet's body for burial — is essentially a single ten-minute shot of Buckley working in near silence. It is the kind of performance that justifies the entire film.

Chloé Zhao's Direction

Zhao films sixteenth-century Stratford the way she filmed contemporary Nevada in Nomadland: handheld, natural light, long takes, and an attention to physical landscape that makes the period feel lived-in rather than reconstructed. There are no establishing shots of London. There is no theatrical Shakespeare melodrama. The film is small, geographically and emotionally, and the better for it.

The Source Material

O'Farrell's novel is one of the most acclaimed historical novels of the last decade. The screenplay (co-written by Zhao and O'Farrell) preserves the book's restraint and its central argument: that grief reorders a life from the inside, and that the public-facing version of a person rarely reveals the private wound that drives them.

Where It Falters

Two consistent critical complaints in the reviews:

  1. Pacing. The film is 138 minutes long, and the middle section (after Hamnet's death, before the writing of Hamlet) sags. Zhao's commitment to letting scenes breathe sometimes tips into letting them stall.
  2. Mescal's underuse. Paul Mescal is excellent in his scenes, but the film is structurally Agnes's story, which leaves Shakespeare as a half-drawn figure. Mescal fans hoping for a showcase will be disappointed.

Neither complaint sinks the film. They do explain why IMDb settled at 7.5 rather than higher — the film is consensus-good but not consensus-great.

Should You Watch It?

Watch it if: you loved Nomadland, you love prestige period drama, you are open to a slow grief film, you want to see the performance that won Best Actress, you are interested in the Shakespeare-as-grieving-father reading.

Skip it if: you found Nomadland inert, you cannot tolerate slow-paced films, you came expecting a Shakespeare biopic with theater scenes, you are not in a place to watch a film about the death of a child.

The film's IMDb 7.5 is honest. It is a good film, exceptionally well-made, with one performance that will be in awards reels for years. It is also a film that asks for patience and gives back something specific in return. If you can give it the runtime, it will give you something back.
