Week 1: June 1–7

June 1: The Catalog Drop

The single most stacked day of the month. Netflix is loading up the catalog with crowd-pleasing classics:

  • Creed (2015), Creed II (2018), Creed III (2023) — the complete Michael B. Jordan trilogy
  • Rocky Balboa (2006) — Stallone's elegiac sixth-round return
  • The Big Lebowski (1998) — the Coens' most-quoted film
  • Bee Movie (2007) — meme immortality, still streaming
  • Cinderella Man (2005) — Ron Howard's Depression-era boxing drama
  • The Chronicles of Riddick (2004) — the Vin Diesel space saga middle chapter

If you only build one weekend queue from this list, do a Creed marathon. The trilogy holds together as one continuous arc better than most franchise sequels, and all three Creed films score above 7.0 on IMDb.

June 3–4: True-Crime and Drama

June 3 brings David, The Hot Seat, and Michael Jackson: The Verdict — the latter is a docuseries revisiting the 2005 trial. June 4 adds The Murder of Rachel Nickell, a British true-crime documentary, plus The Witness.

June 5: Original Movie Day

This is the date to circle. Office Romance stars Jennifer Lopez and Brett Goldstein (yes, Roy Kent himself) as two workaholic colleagues whose mutual hatred slowly thaws. It's directed in the modern Netflix rom-com style — expect a tightly paced 100 minutes.

Mexico 86 is the more interesting bet. Diego Luna plays a sports executive scrambling to host the 1986 World Cup after Colombia drops out. The film leans into the underdog logistics-comedy beats, and Netflix is releasing it the same week the actual 2026 World Cup begins gathering momentum. The timing is deliberate.

Also on June 5: The Marked Woman, Teach You a Lesson, and the J.Lo-Goldstein team-up Voicemails for Isabelle with Zoey Deutch.

June 6: Grey’s Anatomy Season 22

Season 22 picks up the threads from the cliffhanger finale and continues the show's transition era. Grey’s remains one of Netflix's most consistently re-watched library shows, and a fresh season always boosts the back catalog.

Resident Alien Season 4 also drops the same day — Alan Tudyk's tone-perfect comedy is one of the most underrated genre shows on television.

June 7: Poor Things

Yorgos Lanthimos' Poor Things arrives at Netflix after its theatrical run, Hulu window, and awards season. Emma Stone won her second Best Actress Oscar for the lead role, and the film hauled in 11 Academy Award nominations total. IMDb has it at 7.8, Rotten Tomatoes at 92%. If you missed it the first time, this is the month.

Week 2: June 8–14

The second week shifts toward returning series. Every Summer After (technically a Prime Video adaptation, but Netflix has Carley Fortune-adjacent licensed dramas dropping the same week) and several international originals fill out the calendar.

Watch for the mid-month documentary slot: Netflix has consistently used the second week of each month in 2026 to drop a single high-profile true-crime or sports doc.

Week 3: June 15–21

Mid-month is anchored by Netflix's competition slate. Look for the new season of Sweet Magnolias, which has built a quietly loyal audience over four seasons. The romantic-drama crowd will eat this up.

Internationally, expect a major K-drama or Spanish thriller release in this window — Netflix has been front-loading non-English originals in 2026 because they consistently over-index on viewing hours.

Week 4: June 22–30

June 25: Avatar: The Last Airbender Season 2

The biggest release of the second half. Season 1 of the live-action Avatar drew mixed reviews but huge viewership — Netflix renewed it almost immediately for two more seasons. Season 2 covers the Earth Kingdom arc, which includes some of the original animated series' best material: the introduction of Toph, the Ba Sing Se conspiracy, and the buildup to the Day of Black Sun.

If Season 1's tighter pacing and adult-cast adjustments work for you in retrospect, Season 2 is the one to be excited about.

Little Brother

John Cena and Eric André in a Netflix original comedy from director Matt Spicer (Ingrid Goes West). Cena plays a buttoned-up real estate agent whose life implodes when his chaos-agent younger brother (André) moves in. The casting alone makes this worth a trailer-watch.

Leaving Netflix in June 2026

Use the first two weeks to catch anything on your watchlist that's expiring. Notable departures include Kipo and the Age of Wonderbeasts (one of the best original animated series Netflix ever produced) and a rotating batch of older licensed dramas. The whats-on-netflix.com leaving-soon list is the most reliable source.
