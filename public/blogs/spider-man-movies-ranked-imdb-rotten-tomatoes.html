Every Spider-Man Movie Ranked by IMDb & Rotten Tomatoes (2026 Update)

We ranked every theatrical Spider-Man movie — live-action and animated, MCU and Sony — by composite IMDb and Rotten Tomatoes score. The top three: Into the Spider-Verse (2018), Across the Spider-Verse (2023), and Spider-Man: No Way Home (2021). The bottom three: predictably the Sony Spider-Man Universe entries. Spider-Man is the most-adapted live-action superhero of the modern era. Across three actor eras (Tobey Maguire, Andrew Garfield, Tom Holland), two animated franchises (the Spider-Verse films, plus Spider-Noir spinoffs), and one rapidly-collapsing live-action shared universe at Sony, there are now twelve theatrical Spider-Man movies. Some of them are objectively great. Some of them are objectively not. Here is the full ranking.

A few observations before we get into the entries themselves.

What the Numbers Tell Us

The animated Spider-Verse films sit in their own tier. Both Spider-Verse movies score higher than every live-action Spider-Man film by composite. The 92+ tier is rarefied air; almost no superhero films ever get there.

The MCU Holland trilogy is consistent. Homecoming, Far From Home, and No Way Home all sit in the 83–90 range. There is no MCU Spider-Man flop yet, even though the films vary in ambition.

Sam Raimi’s Spider-Man 2 still holds up. Twenty years after release, it is still the highest-rated of the live-action solo films before the MCU era. Audiences and critics agree.

The Sony Spider-Man Universe has cratered. Morbius is one of the lowest-rated theatrical superhero films of the last decade. Venom skates by on tonal clarity. Madame Web and Kraven the Hunter scored even lower in their original theatrical runs and are not included here because they have effectively been removed from the canonical “Spider-Man movie” conversation.

The Top Tier

1. Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse (2018)

The film that broke open what a superhero movie could look and sound like. Phil Lord and Christopher Miller’s production reinvented the visual grammar of animated film — bigger ideas about hand-drawn texture, comic-book pacing, and character-specific frame rates than most studios attempt in a decade. Its 97% Rotten Tomatoes score is one of the highest ever recorded for a comic-book film. Won the Best Animated Feature Oscar. Permanent classic. We have a full ratings page for the sequel here.

2. Spider-Man: Across the Spider-Verse (2023)

The sequel did the impossible: it raised the visual ceiling. Across the Spider-Verse runs four to five distinct animation styles concurrently and somehow makes that legible. The cliffhanger ending split audiences (some loved it, some felt cheated), which is the only reason it sits at #2 instead of #1. The third film, Beyond the Spider-Verse, is currently scheduled for 2027.

3. Spider-Man: No Way Home (2021)

The crossover event most fans assumed would never happen actually happened. Tobey Maguire and Andrew Garfield’s Peter Parkers joined Tom Holland in a film that worked partly on multiverse mechanics and partly on pure fan service. It became the highest-grossing post-pandemic film for over a year and earned an 8.2 IMDb — the highest of any live-action Spider-Man film.

The Strong Middle

4. Spider-Man 2 (2004)

For a long time the gold standard. Doc Ock remains one of the great comic-book film villains. The train-rescue sequence is on every “best superhero movie scenes” list ever written. Some MCU films now score higher by composite, but Raimi’s sequel is still the live-action solo film to beat in pre-MCU territory.

5. Spider-Man: Homecoming (2017)

The MCU integration. Homecoming worked partly because it leaned into John Hughes-esque high school comedy instead of trying to relitigate the origin story. Michael Keaton’s Vulture is one of the MCU’s most quietly excellent villains. Its 7.4 IMDb suggests audience reservations about the Iron Man co-dependency, but the 92% Tomatometer is genuine.

6. Spider-Man: Far From Home (2019)

Less iconic than the bookends but a stronger film than its reputation suggests. Jake Gyllenhaal’s Mysterio is the better-than-it-needed-to-be performance that makes the film’s perception-and-illusion themes land.

7. Spider-Man (2002)

The film that started the modern superhero blockbuster era. Easy to undervalue twenty-plus years on, but its release was an inflection point for the entire genre. Still entirely watchable.

The Mixed Middle

8. The Amazing Spider-Man (2012)

Andrew Garfield’s first outing. Excellent lead performance pulled down by a script that rehashed the origin story too closely after the Raimi version. The Garfield-Stone chemistry is the high point.

9. The Amazing Spider-Man 2 (2014)

The film that ended Sony’s solo Spider-Man era and forced the partnership with Marvel Studios. Visually overstuffed, narratively undercooked. Garfield’s redemption arc would come, retroactively, in No Way Home seven years later.

10. Spider-Man 3 (2007)

The film that ended the Raimi trilogy. Three villains, one symbiote, one dance sequence. Its reputation has softened over the last fifteen years from outright bad to charmingly chaotic, but the composite scores are still middling.

The Bottom

11. Venom (2018)

Tom Hardy’s Venom became a meme franchise. The original film is much weaker than its $856M global gross would suggest, sitting at 30% on Rotten Tomatoes. Its commercial success is part of what kept the Sony Spider-Man Universe alive longer than the quality justified.

12. Morbius (2022)

One of the most-mocked superhero films of the modern era. The “It’s Morbin’ Time” meme cycle far outlasted the film’s theatrical run. 15% Rotten Tomatoes. 5.2 IMDb. The composite score speaks for itself.

How to Watch in Order

If you have never seen any of these films and want a guided run, here is a clean watch order:

  1. Raimi trilogy — Spider-Man (2002), Spider-Man 2 (2004), Spider-Man 3 (2007). Skip 3 if you only have time for one.
  2. Holland MCU trilogy — Homecoming, Far From Home, No Way Home (in release order).
  3. Spider-Verse films — Into the Spider-Verse, Across the Spider-Verse.
  4. Optional: The Amazing Spider-Man films for full Garfield context before No Way Home.

Skip the Sony Spider-Man Universe entries unless you want the full picture.
