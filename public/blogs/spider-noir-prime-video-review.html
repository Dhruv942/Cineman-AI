Spider-Noir on Prime Video: Release Date, Cast & What to Expect (May 2026)

Updated: April 24, 2026 7 min read

TL;DR

Spider-Noir premieres on Prime Video on May 27, 2026. It is a live-action period series set in 1930s New York City — separate from the MCU. The series adapts the comic book Spider-Man Noir character, known to wider audiences from Into the Spider-Verse. One of the more unconventional superhero projects in streaming this year.

Spider-Noir is the kind of superhero project that would have seemed unlikely a few years ago: a live-action period drama set in Depression-era New York, built around one of Marvel's most idiosyncratic alternate-universe characters. But after the enormous success of Into the Spider-Verse and its sequel validated appetite for non-standard Spider-Man stories, Amazon's Prime Video has made a substantial bet on the concept. The series arrives May 27, 2026.

Release Date and Format

Spider-Noir premieres on Prime Video on May 27, 2026. The release format — full season drop versus weekly episodes — had not been confirmed as of mid-April 2026. Given that Prime Video has used both formats for different originals recently (Citadel Season 2 goes weekly; Good Omens Season 3 is a full drop), either is possible. The series is expected to run eight to ten episodes for its first season.

What is Spider-Noir?

Spider-Man Noir is a Marvel Comics character introduced in the 2009 limited series Spider-Man Noir. The concept: Peter Parker in an alternate 1933 New York City, where the Great Depression has created a darker, more violent city. This version of Peter is a photographer for a socialist newspaper who gains spider powers after being bitten by a spider that emerged from a mystical spider statue. The character operates in the visual language of 1930s pulp fiction — fedora, trench coat, black-and-white aesthetic — and deals with gangsters and corruption rather than cosmic threats.

Broader mainstream audiences encountered Spider-Man Noir in Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse (2018), where he was voiced by Nicolas Cage as a comic relief character who could not understand color television and spoke in hardboiled detective clichés. That interpretation was deliberately comedic. The Prime Video series appears to take a more straight-faced approach to the period noir concept.

The Setting: 1930s New York

The period setting is what makes Spider-Noir genuinely distinct from every other superhero project in streaming. Depression-era New York as a backdrop is cinematically rich territory: gangster empires, union organizing, immigration, poverty, corruption, and a city in the process of defining its modern identity. That canvas gives Spider-Noir storytelling material that no contemporary-set superhero show can access.

The visual reference points the production has cited publicly include classic film noir like Chinatown, The Maltese Falcon, and L.A. Confidential, filtered through the heightened aesthetics of superhero storytelling. The costume design challenge — making a period-accurate 1930s Spider-Man costume feel plausible rather than silly — is one of the production's most visible creative hurdles.

How Spider-Noir Fits Into the Sony/Marvel Landscape

The licensing situation for Spider-Man content is unusually complex. Sony Pictures owns the film rights to Spider-Man and all associated characters. The main MCU Spider-Man (Tom Holland) exists under a co-production deal between Sony and Disney/Marvel. Sony has separately licensed specific Spider-Man characters to streaming platforms for standalone projects — Spider-Noir being one of them, Silk (also at Amazon) being another.

This means Spider-Noir exists in its own continuity, separate from the MCU. It can use the Spider-Man Noir character and his specific villains and supporting cast, but not MCU characters like Tony Stark, Nick Fury, or any of the MCU Avengers. For the period-set noir concept, this is not a limitation — none of those MCU characters belong in 1933 anyway.

Why Spider-Noir Is One of the More Interesting Superhero Projects of 2026

Superhero fatigue is a real and well-documented phenomenon in 2026. Audiences have become significantly more selective about which cape-and-cowl projects they invest in, following years of declining MCU film performance and mixed responses to Disney+ superhero series. The projects that have cut through recently — Invincible, The Boys, the Spider-Verse films — have all done so by doing something genuinely different with the genre's conventions.

Spider-Noir belongs in that category of "doing something different." A 1930s period drama as superhero vehicle is a legitimate formal experiment, not a gimmick. The question is whether the execution matches the concept. Pre-release tracking shows significant curiosity but appropriate caution — the concept is compelling but the execution is unproven. What We Know About the Plot

Plot details have been kept under relatively tight embargo. What has been confirmed from official synopsis materials:

  • The series is set in 1933 New York City at the height of the Great Depression
  • Peter Parker is an investigative photographer working the crime beat
  • The central conflict involves organized crime and corruption in city government
  • The series leans into the hardboiled detective/mystery genre alongside its superhero elements
  • The visual approach is deliberately desaturated and period-specific

The villain roster — who Spider-Man Noir faces in the comics includes the Goblin (a very different version of the character) and the Vulture — has not been officially confirmed for the series. The production has kept villain reveals for post-premiere audience discovery.

Prime Video's May 2026 Superhero Context

Spider-Noir is Prime Video's second significant superhero property of May 2026, following Citadel Season 2 (which has espionage and action superhero adjacency) on May 6. It also comes a few months after Invincible Season 3 on Prime Video earlier in 2026. Amazon has established itself as a viable home for non-MCU superhero content. Spider-Noir will be a significant test of how much appetite exists for period-specific superhero storytelling specifically.
