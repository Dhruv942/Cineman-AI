Back to the Future is a hard movie to find a successor for. It does at least four things genuinely well at the same time: a fun, internally-consistent time-travel premise; a terrific buddy chemistry; a tight family drama at its emotional core; and a propulsive third act that pays off everything the first hour set up. Most films that try to be "the next Back to the Future" pick one of those threads and miss the others.

Below are 15 films that hit at least two of the four. We ranked them by combining IMDb user scores, Rotten Tomatoes critic scores, and TMDB audience scores into a composite ranking.

Pick by What You Love About Back to the Future

If you love the time-travel mechanics

Looper, Predestination, Source Code, and Edge of Tomorrow are the most internally rigorous picks. Looper plays loose with paradoxes but commits hard to its world. Predestination is built around a single time-travel paradox so elegant it has launched a thousand explainer videos. Source Code keeps its mechanics simple but uses them to brilliant character effect. Edge of Tomorrow is the most fun — a video-game logic action film where every restart sharpens the protagonist's instincts.

If you love the buddy chemistry

The Princess Bride, Hot Tub Time Machine, Bill & Ted, and The Adam Project. The Doc-and-Marty dynamic is one of cinema's great odd-couple pairings, and these films build their stories around comparable two-handers. Bill & Ted is the most direct comedic descendant. The Adam Project (Ryan Reynolds and his younger self) is the most direct narrative descendant.

If you love the family-drama core

Frequency, About Time, Big, and Hook. These are the picks that understand what Back to the Future was actually about: how seeing your parents as people, not parents, changes you. Frequency in particular captures that father-son emotional engine in a way few other time travel films have.

If you love the propulsive comedy

Groundhog Day, Palm Springs, and Edge of Tomorrow. These films understand that the most fun thing about time travel is the comedic potential of repetition. Groundhog Day is the canonical version. Palm Springs is its sharpest modern update.

Why Back to the Future Holds Up After 40 Years

Most blockbuster franchises age into nostalgia objects. Back to the Future ages into a textbook. The script is taught in screenwriting courses because every setup pays off, every joke has a structural purpose, and the third-act climax (Marty has to be born for him to exist) is mechanically perfect.

The films on this list are the ones that learned the most from that script. Some learned the time-travel craft (Looper, Predestination). Some learned the buddy comedy (Bill & Ted, The Adam Project). Some learned the heart (Frequency, About Time). The few that learned all three — Groundhog Day, Edge of Tomorrow, About Time — sit at the top of this list for a reason.
