The Devil Wears Prada at 20: Why It’s Still Pop Culture Royalty

Updated: May 9, 2026 11 min read

TL;DR

The Devil Wears Prada turns 20 in summer 2026. Two decades after its release, the IMDb rating sits at 6.9, the Rotten Tomatoes Tomatometer at 75%, and the audience score at 84%. None of those numbers capture how culturally durable this film actually is. With The Devil Wears Prada 2 hitting theaters on May 1, 2026, here’s why the original is still the working girl movie. It is a curious thing about The Devil Wears Prada: by the numbers, it is a perfectly good comedy. A 6.9 IMDb. A 75% on Rotten Tomatoes. Numbers you would expect from a glossy mid-2000s adaptation of a chick-lit bestseller. What the numbers cannot capture is the cultural footprint. Twenty years after release, this is still the film that runs in office breakrooms, gets quoted on TikTok by people who were toddlers when it premiered, and dictates exactly what a power-suit boss villain is supposed to look and sound like.

With The Devil Wears Prada 2 in theaters now, this is a good moment to ask: how did a competently-rated 2006 comedy become genuine pop-culture canon?

Why the Original Still Works

1. Meryl Streep’s Miranda Priestly Is Permanent

Streep’s performance is the structural reason this film outlives its ratings. She makes Miranda Priestly a fully three-dimensional villain: terrifying, but also tired, smart, lonely, professionally singular. The cerulean speech alone — the scene where Miranda dresses down Andy for laughing at fashion — is one of the most-quoted monologues in a 21st-century studio comedy. Streep got the only Oscar nomination of the film for it (her 14th, with two more wins still to come). The performance is good enough that it has retroactively elevated the surrounding film.

2. Anne Hathaway Was the Right Anchor

Hathaway plays Andy Sachs as a young woman who walks into Runway as a self-described serious journalist and slowly, almost against her will, lets the world take her over. The arc requires her to be a believable straight-arrow at the start and a believable fashion-world insider by the midpoint. Hathaway pulls off the swap so smoothly that audiences barely register the transformation as performance.

3. Stanley Tucci’s Nigel Steals Every Scene

Nigel is the tertiary character who became, for many viewers, the true emotional center of the film. Tucci plays him with a wry, tired kindness that contrasts beautifully with Streep’s ice and Emily Blunt’s neuroses. His “you have no style, no sense of fashion” speech to Andy is the second-most-quoted moment in the film.

4. Emily Blunt’s Star Was Made Here

Emily Blunt was a relative unknown in 2006. The performance as Emily Charlton — the snippy, ambitious, perpetually exhausted senior assistant — was star-making. Blunt has subsequently anchored everything from Edge of Tomorrow to A Quiet Place to Oppenheimer. Watching her in Devil Wears Prada twenty years on, the reasons are obvious.

5. The Script Is Sneakier Than It Looks

Aline Brosh McKenna’s screenplay reads as a glossy fashion-world comedy on the surface. Underneath, it is a fairly serious meditation on ambition, identity, and the cost of professional success. The film does not flinch from showing that Miranda’s success has hollowed out her personal life. It also does not pretend that Andy’s rejection of that path makes her morally superior. That moral ambiguity is part of why the film keeps getting reinterpreted.

Why It Plays Differently in 2026

One thing that has happened since 2006: a generation of viewers has reread The Devil Wears Prada as a film about a competent woman who refused to apologize for her competence. Miranda Priestly has been steadily upgraded from villain to ambivalent hero in the cultural consensus — a function of two decades of think pieces, a TikTok generation hostile to bossy-woman framings, and probably a quiet shift in how audiences think about female ambition in workplaces.

The 6.9 IMDb has not really moved during that reinterpretation. But the film itself is doing different work for the audience now than it was doing in 2006. That is rare for a comedy.

The Devil Wears Prada 2: What to Expect

The sequel hit theaters May 1, 2026, with both Streep and Hathaway returning, alongside David Frankel reprising his director role. Plot details have been kept tight, but here is what is confirmed:

  • The story picks up roughly twenty years after the original.
  • Miranda is still at Runway. The magazine industry has collapsed around her.
  • Andy is no longer in journalism; her exact career arc is part of the sequel’s reveal.
  • Stanley Tucci returns as Nigel.
  • Emily Blunt’s involvement was teased in marketing but not confirmed.

Bottom Line

The 6.9 IMDb does not lie, but it does not tell the whole story either. The Devil Wears Prada is one of those films where the cultural footprint outpaced the score within five years and never looked back. It made stars of three of its leads, gave us a permanent fictional villain, and continues to reward rewatch viewing twenty years on. With the sequel now in theaters, this is the rare anniversary that has actually generated new conversation rather than just nostalgic ones. Watch the original first if you have not in a while. It still works.
