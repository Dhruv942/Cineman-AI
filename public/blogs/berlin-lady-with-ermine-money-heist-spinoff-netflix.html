Netflix's most resilient Spanish franchise is back. Berlin and the Lady with an Ermine, the second season of the Money Heist spinoff centered on Pedro Alonso's elegant, terminally ill master thief, premieres globally on Friday, May 15, 2026. After the first Berlin season planted its flag in Paris, this run trades the Louvre's shadows for Seville's sun-bleached plazas, with a heist that is more pretext than purpose.

Here is everything we know about the cast, plot, episode count, where it sits on the Money Heist timeline, and whether it is worth your weekend.

What Is Berlin and the Lady with an Ermine About?

The official Netflix logline: Berlin reunites his crew in Seville, Spain, where they pretend to plan the theft of Leonardo da Vinci's Lady with an Ermine, the famous Renaissance portrait. The painting is a cover. The real target is revenge against the Duke of Malaga, a wealthy Andalusian aristocrat who, in Berlin's words, "took something he should not have."

Showrunners Alex Pina and Esther Martinez Lobato (the original Money Heist creators) have leaned harder into the caper-comedy register that defined Berlin season one. Less hostage-crisis tension, more Ocean's Eleven swagger. The art-world setting also lets the show flex production design in a way the bank-vault and Bank of Spain settings of the parent series never quite did.

The Crew

Most of Berlin's Paris team is back:

  • Berlin (Pedro Alonso) — the brain, the ego, the man with nothing left to lose.
  • Damian (Tristan Ulloa) — Berlin's old friend and the moral counterweight to his recklessness.
  • Keila (Michelle Jenner) — the hacker whose self-doubt was a quiet engine of season one.
  • Bruce (Joel Sanchez) — the muscle, now navigating the messy fallout of his Paris romance.
  • Cameron (Begona Vargas) — the chaos agent, still volatile.
  • Roi (Julio Pena) — Berlin's loyal apprentice, increasingly sure of himself.

New additions for the Seville run include Itziar Ituno in a recurring role (yes, you may recognize her from somewhere) and a Spanish character actor playing the Duke whom early reviews are already calling the franchise's best villain since the Inspectora Murillo days.

How It Connects to Money Heist

The Berlin spinoff is set before the events of Money Heist Part 5, while Berlin is still alive and not yet recruited into the Royal Mint of Spain plot. That gives the spinoff a built-in tragic undercurrent: we know how Berlin's story ends. Every grand romantic gesture, every act of theatrical cruelty, is shaded by the fact that the man does not get a happy ending.

You do not need to have watched Money Heist to enjoy this season — the series works as a standalone caper. But fans will pick up small references to Berlin's marriages, his medical condition, and the Professor (whose presence haunts the edges of the story without ever showing up directly).

Tone and Pacing

If the parent show was a pressure cooker, Berlin is a soufflé. The first season opted for romance, slick set-pieces, and a lighter touch with the violence. Season two pushes that even further. The Seville setting saturates everything in golden hour, and the dialogue trades the Professor's whiteboard monologues for Berlin's florid, frequently funny self-mythologizing.

That said, do not expect a comedy. The vendetta plot turns dark in the second half, and the Duke's storyline carries genuine moral weight. Pina has said in interviews that he wanted the season to feel like "a love letter and a knife at the same time."

Episode Guide

Berlin and the Lady with an Ermine is eight episodes, all dropping May 15. Total runtime is approximately seven hours. Here is a non-spoiler breakdown of the structure:

  • Episodes 1-2: Setup. Berlin assembles the crew in Seville, lays out the Lady with an Ermine plan, and we meet the Duke.
  • Episodes 3-4: The crew runs the cover heist while the real plan begins to emerge in fragments.
  • Episodes 5-6: A betrayal forces Berlin to improvise. This is the season's tonal pivot.
  • Episodes 7-8: The Duke confrontation, the painting, and a finale that resets the table for a potential season three.

Critical Reception (Early)

Early reviews from Spanish and European outlets are warm but not ecstatic. The consensus: the production design is sumptuous, Alonso's performance is the franchise's anchor, and the season is more emotionally satisfying than season one even if it does not quite hit the parent show's narrative highs.

Expect IMDb to settle around the high 7s once enough viewers weigh in — a tier above many Netflix originals but below Money Heist Parts 1 and 2's lofty 8.2. Should You Watch It?

If you loved Berlin season one, this is an easy yes. If you bounced off it because you missed the Professor or wanted higher stakes, the Seville season probably will not change your mind — the Berlin universe is Berlin's, and he runs it on charm, not desperation.

For Money Heist fans on the fence: the references and tonal continuity make it worth at least the first two episodes. By episode three you will know whether the Berlin spinoff is your show or not. The franchise has earned the right to a long evening on your couch.

Frequently Asked Questions

When does Berlin and the Lady with an Ermine premiere?

It premieres globally on Netflix on May 15, 2026, with all eight episodes dropping at once in the standard Netflix binge-release format.

Do I need to watch Money Heist before this spinoff?

It helps but is not strictly required. The series is set before the events of Money Heist Part 5, so it works as a standalone caper. Money Heist fans will catch more references to Berlin's backstory and his crew dynamics.

Is Pedro Alonso returning as Berlin?

Yes. Pedro Alonso reprises his role as Andres de Fonollosa, alias Berlin, the suave, terminally-ill thief whose flair and ruthlessness made him one of Money Heist's most beloved characters.

How long is each episode?

Episodes run approximately 50-60 minutes each, in keeping with the Money Heist universe pacing. The season contains eight episodes, totaling roughly seven hours of content.
