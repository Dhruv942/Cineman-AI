Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince IMDb Rating Explained (7.6/10)

Updated: May 29, 2026 10 min read

TL;DR

Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince holds a 7.6 IMDb rating — the lowest in the eight-film franchise. Critics liked it (84% RT). Audiences felt key material was cut, the tone leaned too heavily into teen romance, and the climactic Astronomy Tower sequence missed crucial source material. A 7.6 IMDb rating is, objectively, a strong score. It places Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince above hundreds of well-regarded films. But in the context of the Harry Potter series — where Deathly Hallows: Part 2 hits 8.1 and Prisoner of Azkaban reaches 7.9 — the sixth installment sits in last place. Understanding why requires looking at the gap between what the source material delivered and what the film chose to compress.

The 7.6 ties with Sorcerer's Stone, but Half-Blood Prince is widely considered the franchise's weakest entry by fan polls and Letterboxd rankings. Why does it underperform expectations when the raw rating is decent?

Reason 1: Massive Source-Material Cuts

The novel Harry Potter and the Half-Blood Prince is essentially the prologue to Deathly Hallows. It contains the entire backstory for Voldemort that the final book depends on: the Gaunt family, the orphanage flashbacks, Tom Riddle's Hogwarts years, the systematic creation of the Horcruxes. The book also opens with a massive sequence — the Battle at the Burrow — that establishes the war is no longer theoretical.

The film cuts roughly 70% of the Voldemort backstory. It includes only two of the half-dozen Pensieve flashbacks. The Burrow sequence is replaced with a much smaller incident. The most critical scene in the book — Harry's deep dive into how Voldemort created his Horcruxes — is replaced with a single Slughorn confession. The result: viewers who hadn't read the books often arrived at Deathly Hallows: Part 1 with no idea why the trio was suddenly hunting Horcruxes.

Reason 2: The Tone Pivots Hard Into Romantic Comedy

Director David Yates and screenwriter Steve Kloves leaned heavily into the year's romantic entanglements. The Harry-Ginny, Ron-Lavender, Ron-Hermione triangle, and Hermione-Cormac dynamics consume large portions of the runtime. The book balances those scenes against the Voldemort backstory and the war's escalation. The film does not.

Some of it works. Ron and Lavender's relationship is the film's comedic highlight. Hermione's heartbroken “Don't — don't” in the staircase scene is one of Emma Watson's best moments in the entire series. But the tonal balance is off. The audience came to see how the war was about to start. They got an hour of teen romance and 30 minutes of plot.

Reason 3: The Astronomy Tower Sequence Lost Its Source Material

The film's climactic sequence is structurally identical to the book: Snape kills Dumbledore on the Astronomy Tower. But the book sets it up across hundreds of pages of accumulated evidence about Snape's loyalty, the Unbreakable Vow with Narcissa, and the careful seeding of Snape's actual mission. The film keeps the action and removes much of the setup. The result: a moment that should land as a devastating reveal lands as a confusing twist for non-readers, and a less-impactful echo for readers.

The book also famously ends with the Battle of Hogwarts — not the final battle, but a chaotic Death Eater raid on the school in the immediate aftermath of Dumbledore's death. The film cuts this entirely. It's a defensible production choice (the budget went toward Deathly Hallows) but it deflates the ending.

What the Film Does Well

In fairness: Half-Blood Prince has the best cinematography in the franchise. Bruno Delbonnel's washed-out, almost monochromatic palette gives the film a visual identity none of the others match. The cave sequence in the third act is the most genuinely horrifying scene in the series. Jim Broadbent's Slughorn is a perfect addition. Tom Felton finally gets material worthy of his three-film-long Draco buildup. Alan Rickman's Snape is doing some of the most controlled work of his career, even with reduced screen time.

If the film weren't carrying the weight of being the bridge to the final two installments, the 7.6 rating would feel correct. It's a well-made movie. It's a partial adaptation that left too much on the table.

The Critic vs. Audience Split

Half-Blood Prince's 84% Rotten Tomatoes critics score is the third-highest in the franchise. Critics evaluating the film as a standalone work largely praised its visual ambition and emotional maturity. The audience score (78%) and IMDb (7.6) reflect the franchise-context evaluation: this is the film that was supposed to set up the ending, and it didn't fully do its job.

The pattern is unusual: Half-Blood Prince is rated higher than franchise average by critics and Metacritic, but lower by audiences and IMDb voters. That's a near-perfect signature of a film that succeeds on craft and falters on franchise-narrative obligations.
