Why Forrest Gump Has an 8.8 IMDb Rating: The 1994 Classic Explained

Updated: May 29, 2026 11 min read

TL;DR

Forrest Gump's 8.8 IMDb rating puts it in the all-time top 15 films — ahead of The Lord of the Rings, Casablanca, and most Best Picture winners ever made. The combination of Tom Hanks's career-defining performance, Eric Roth's script, Robert Zemeckis's invisible visual-effects craft, and a perfectly-pitched soundtrack created a film audiences have voted on consistently for three decades. An 8.8 IMDb rating sounds modest until you look at where it sits. Only 14 films in IMDb history have ever held above 8.8 with at least one million votes. Forrest Gump is one of them. It ranks above Inception at 8.8 (with which it ties), above The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers at 8.8, above almost every Spielberg film, and ahead of all but three Best Picture winners in cinematic history.

How? The film is from 1994. It is overtly sentimental. It has been parodied for thirty years. And it still holds. The answer is in the craft. Below is the breakdown.

Reason 1: Tom Hanks Gave the Performance of a Generation

Hanks won the Best Actor Oscar in 1994 for Philadelphia. He won it again in 1995 for Forrest Gump. Only one other actor in Academy history had ever won consecutive Best Actor Oscars (Spencer Tracy, in 1937 and 1938). Hanks did it with two completely different performances: an AIDS lawyer in Philadelphia, a low-IQ Alabama every-man in Forrest Gump.

The Forrest performance is the harder lift. The character could collapse into caricature in a hundred small ways — the accent, the cadence, the running gag — and Hanks never lets it. Forrest is treated as a complete human being whose perceptions are genuinely his own. The audience never laughs at him. Three decades of imitators have failed to land the same tonal balance.

Reason 2: The Script Pulled Off an Impossible Structure

Eric Roth adapted Winston Groom's 1986 novel into something almost completely different. The novel's Forrest is sharper, sarcastic, and the plot is rougher — he goes to space, gets stranded with cannibals, takes a job as a wrestler. Roth threw out the absurdist material and rebuilt the structure around a single device: Forrest as the accidental witness to American history from 1955 to 1982.

The genius is the framing. Every major American cultural moment of those decades — Elvis, Vietnam, Watergate, the ping-pong-diplomacy China opening, the AIDS crisis, the runner subculture of the late 70s — happens to Forrest. He shakes JFK's hand. He's at the Lincoln Memorial march. He invents the smiley face on accident. The script gets away with showing you American history as a montage because Forrest is the literal vehicle. Almost any other character at the center of this conceit would feel like cheap nostalgia. Forrest's emotional simplicity earns it.

Reason 3: Zemeckis’s Visual Effects Were Invisible

The visual effects in Forrest Gump won the Oscar for a reason most viewers don't realize: nearly every “historical” sequence inserting Forrest into real footage was a then-unprecedented digital composite. JFK, Nixon, John Lennon — all reconstituted from archival footage using CGI mouth-replacement techniques that effectively invented modern face-replacement VFX. Lieutenant Dan's legs were a different breakthrough: Gary Sinise's actual legs were digitally erased frame by frame, six years before The Lord of the Rings made digital character work mainstream.

You don't notice any of this. That's the point. The effects are completely invisible because they exist to support the story rather than to perform their own existence. That craft holds up in 2026 better than most CGI from films made fifteen years later.

Reason 4: The Soundtrack Did Half the Work

The double-CD soundtrack went 12x platinum. The film uses 32 songs spanning 1956 to 1979 — Elvis, Dylan, Joan Baez, Creedence, Lynyrd Skynyrd, the Doors, Aretha Franklin, Simon & Garfunkel, Jimi Hendrix, Fleetwood Mac. Every musical cue is doing emotional and historical work simultaneously. The 1968 Vietnam montage scored to “Fortunate Son”? That association exists in the cultural memory almost entirely because of this film. Same with “Free Bird” over the Lieutenant Dan scenes.

Alan Silvestri's original score, particularly the “Feather Theme,” is the connective tissue. It's one of the most recognizable two-minute themes in film history, doing in a piano motif what the script does in dialogue.

Reason 5: It Tells One Clean Emotional Story

Strip away the historical-cameo framework, and Forrest Gump is one of the simplest love stories ever filmed. A boy meets a girl on a school bus. He loves her for the rest of his life. She is damaged in ways he doesn't fully understand. She leaves and returns and leaves again. He waits. She dies. He raises their son. That's it.

The emotional spine is clean enough for an eight-year-old to track and complex enough for an adult to find new texture in across rewatches. The bench-monologue framing device gives the whole film the rhythm of a story being told, which is why so many sequences feel etched into cultural memory after a single viewing.

The Critic-Audience Split, Honestly

The 71% Rotten Tomatoes critics score versus the 95% audience score reflects a real debate. Critics — particularly in the years since — have argued the film's politics are conservative-leaning (Forrest succeeds through passivity; Jenny's countercultural choices punish her), that its sentimentality is unearned, and that its competition at the 1995 Oscars (Pulp Fiction, The Shawshank Redemption) was more formally ambitious.

The audience read isn't naive. Audiences respond to the craft, the performances, and the emotional payoff. IMDb's 8.8 reflects three decades of voters making that case in the only language IMDb has: a number.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the IMDb rating for Forrest Gump?

8.8 out of 10 from over 2.3 million votes. It ranks roughly #12 on IMDb's all-time Top 250.

Why is Forrest Gump rated so highly on IMDb but lower on Rotten Tomatoes?

Critic-audience split. Critics have argued the politics are conservative-leaning and the sentimentality is unearned. Audiences respond to Hanks's performance and the emotional payoff. IMDb weighs audience response, hence the 8.8.

Did Forrest Gump win Best Picture?

Yes. It won 6 Oscars in 1995: Best Picture, Best Director, Best Actor (Hanks), Best Adapted Screenplay, Best Film Editing, and Best Visual Effects. It beat Pulp Fiction and The Shawshank Redemption.
