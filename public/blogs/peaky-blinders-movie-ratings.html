Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man — IMDb, RT Ratings & Streaming Guide

Updated: March 31, 2026 11 min read

TL;DR

The Peaky Blinders franchise has finally made the leap from television to film, and by the order of the ratings aggregators, it has done so successfully. Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man arrived on Netflix in March 2026, bringing Cillian Murphy back as Tommy Shelby for what creator Steven Knight has described as the definitive end of the character's story. The film carries a 7.7 on IMDb and a 91% critics score on Rotten Tomatoes — numbers that suggest the transition from small screen to big screen has been handled with care.

But how do these numbers stack up against the series? Is the movie a worthy send-off for one of television's most iconic characters? And what should you watch next if you are craving more gritty crime drama? Let us break it all down.

At first glance, the movie's 7.7 IMDb might look like a step down from the series' overall 8.8 rating. But there is important context here. TV series on IMDb benefit from sustained engagement — loyal fans rate every season, and the cumulative score reflects years of investment. A standalone film does not get that same runway. A 7.7 for a TV-to-film adaptation is actually excellent; most fall in the 5.5 to 6.5 range.

The 91% Rotten Tomatoes score is the more telling figure. It means that the vast majority of professional critics gave the film a positive review, which is a strong endorsement for a franchise that could easily have stumbled in the transition to a cinematic format. The critical consensus praises the film's ambition, its visual scope, and Murphy's performance, while noting that the compressed runtime means some storylines feel less developed than they would have in a full season.

How It Compares to the Series

The Peaky Blinders TV series ran for six seasons between 2013 and 2022, and its quality was not uniform. Seasons 2 through 4 are generally considered the peak, with individual episodes regularly hitting 9.0+ on IMDb. Season 6, which was filmed under pandemic restrictions and without the late Helen McCrory, is widely regarded as the weakest, though still strong by most standards.

The Immortal Man sits comfortably in the upper tier of the franchise. It does not reach the heights of the best episodes from Seasons 2 or 3, but it surpasses much of Season 6 and delivers a more satisfying conclusion than the series finale did. For fans who felt that the TV ending left things unresolved, the movie provides the closure that was missing.

Director Tom Harper, who directed several episodes of the series, brings a cinematic sensibility that elevates the material. The film feels genuinely like a movie rather than an extended TV episode, with wider compositions, more deliberate pacing, and set pieces that would not have been possible on a television budget. At the same time, it retains the show's signature atmosphere: the slow-motion walks, the period-anachronistic soundtrack, and the smoke-filled rooms where deals are made and broken.

Cast and Creative Team

Cillian Murphy leads the film as Tommy Shelby, delivering a performance that critics have called his best in the role. The supporting cast includes returning favorites — Sophie Rundle as Ada, Paul Anderson as Arthur — alongside new additions including Tim Roth and Rebecca Ferguson, both of whom bring weight to their respective roles.

Steven Knight wrote the screenplay, maintaining his grip on the world he created over a decade ago. The film is set in the late 1930s, as the shadow of World War II falls over Birmingham and the Shelby empire. Without venturing into spoiler territory, the story balances the political machinations that defined the later seasons with the personal, family-driven drama that made the early seasons so compelling.

The Verdict

Peaky Blinders: The Immortal Man is a strong film and a fitting conclusion to one of television's most distinctive franchises. A 7.7 IMDb and 91% Rotten Tomatoes score place it well above the average for TV-to-film adaptations, and critics are nearly unanimous that Cillian Murphy delivers a performance worth watching. It is not perfect — the compressed runtime means some threads feel underserved — but as a send-off for Tommy Shelby, it delivers.
