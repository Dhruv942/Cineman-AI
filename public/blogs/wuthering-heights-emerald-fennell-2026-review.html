Wuthering Heights (2026): Emerald Fennell's Polarizing Reinvention

Emerald Fennell's third feature is the kind of project that was never going to land quietly. Wuthering Heights is one of the most adapted novels in the English language — and Fennell, who built her career on stylish provocation, was always going to make the version that argues with all the others.

After a limited theatrical run earlier this spring, Wuthering Heights is now available on HBO Max in the US. Here is what to expect.

What Is the 2026 Wuthering Heights?

The plot is recognizable: a foundling boy named Heathcliff is taken in by the Earnshaw family on the Yorkshire moors. He forms an obsessive bond with Catherine, the family's daughter. When Catherine marries the wealthy Edgar Linton instead of him, Heathcliff disappears, returns rich and consumed by spite, and methodically destroys both families across two generations.

Fennell keeps the bones. What she changes is everything else. Her Heights is a film of extreme close-ups, candy-colored lighting, anachronistic music cues (yes, there are needle drops), and explicit sexuality that the novel only gestures at. The moors are less windswept ruin and more music-video swirl. The Yorkshire dialect is pared back. The interior monologues that drive the book are replaced by lingering looks and ambient menace.

The Cast

  • Margot Robbie as Catherine Earnshaw — the film's gravitational center. Robbie's Catherine is more knowing and less ethereal than past versions.
  • Jacob Elordi as Heathcliff — cast for his stillness as much as his height. Elordi plays Heathcliff as a wound that never heals.
  • Hong Chau as Nelly Dean — the housekeeper-narrator, here repositioned as something closer to a witness who saw everything and chose to tell.
  • Owen Cooper (the young breakout from Adolescence) as the young Hindley Earnshaw — a brief but devastating performance.
  • Charissa Bannon as Isabella Linton — the season's most unexpectedly affecting subplot.

How It Compares to Previous Adaptations

There have been roughly a dozen serious Wuthering Heights adaptations on screen. The most famous:

  • 1939 (William Wyler): Laurence Olivier and Merle Oberon. Romantic, simplified, ends halfway through the novel.
  • 1992 (Peter Kosminsky): Ralph Fiennes and Juliette Binoche. Faithful, stately, slightly airless.
  • 2011 (Andrea Arnold): Kaya Scodelario and James Howson. Stripped-down, naturalistic, and the first to cast a Black actor as Heathcliff — an interpretation many critics consider definitive.
  • 2026 (Emerald Fennell): Robbie and Elordi. Provocative, stylized, divisive.

If Andrea Arnold's 2011 version was the most rigorous adaptation, Fennell's 2026 take is the most argumentative. It is in conversation with the novel rather than serving it. Whether that excites you or annoys you will probably determine your entire response to the film.

The Fennell Aesthetic, Now Period

Promising Young Woman applied the candy-pink palette to a revenge thriller. Saltburn applied it to country-house gothic. Wuthering Heights applies it to the Bronte canon, and the results are immediately recognizable: glossy surfaces, sharp dialogue, sex that lingers on the line between erotic and grotesque, and an unwillingness to let any scene end gracefully.

Fennell has talked in interviews about wanting to "remind people that this book is unhinged." She has a point — Wuthering Heights, the novel, is far stranger and more violent than its romantic-canon reputation suggests. Whether her stylistic choices honor that strangeness or flatten it into a vibe is the central critical question of the film.

Critical Reception

Reviews are split right down the middle. The Guardian called it "deliriously committed to its own bad taste." The New York Times found it "a film at war with the book it claims to love." Letterboxd is, predictably, a battlefield. Audiences are responding more warmly than critics, which is the standard Fennell pattern.

Should You Watch It?

You will know within ten minutes. If the opening sequence (which leans hard into the gothic horror that has always lurked under the novel's surface) thrills you, you are in for the ride. If it feels like provocation for its own sake, the next two hours will be a slog.

Watch it if: you loved Saltburn, you have always thought Wuthering Heights was secretly a horror story, you enjoy formal experimentation, you think Margot Robbie is at her best when given dangerous material.

Skip it if: you want a faithful Bronte adaptation, you found Saltburn empty, you are sensitive to graphic sexuality and violence, or you simply do not enjoy films that argue with their source material.
