Why Spider-Man: Across the Spider-Verse Has an 8.5 IMDb Rating

Updated: May 29, 2026 11 min read

TL;DR

Spider-Man: Across the Spider-Verse holds an 8.5 IMDb rating and 95% Rotten Tomatoes, placing it among the highest-rated animated films of the 2020s. Six distinct animation styles, a 240-shot stylistic breakthrough per sequence, Phil Lord and Chris Miller's script, and one of the most-debated cliffhanger endings of the decade all explain why. An 8.5 IMDb rating with over 500,000 votes is rare for any film. For an animated sequel released only five years after the original, it is almost unheard of. Spider-Man: Across the Spider-Verse earned it. The film grossed $690 million worldwide on a $100 million budget, won every major Annie Award, and has held its rating remarkably steady since its 2023 release — usually a sign that audiences are still finding new texture in repeat viewings.

Reason 1: The Animation Itself Is a Breakthrough

Across the Spider-Verse uses six different animation styles, switching between them based on which Spider-character is on screen or which dimension the camera is in. Miles Morales's Brooklyn is rendered in the same comic-book-halftone style that Into the Spider-Verse pioneered. Gwen Stacy's universe uses watercolor backgrounds that shift in real-time with her emotional state — the colors literally bleeding into different palettes during her conversations. Pavitr Prabhakar's Mumbattan combines 3D animation with Hindu temple architectural patterns hand-painted into every shot. Spider-Punk is collage-style, with each frame composed like a different ransom-note assembly.

The technical lift was unprecedented. Reports during production indicated that some sequences took two years of work for a single 30-second scene. The film's frame rate variations — characters animated at 12 frames per second versus 24 fps depending on context — create a tactile feel that audiences and critics have repeatedly called “the future of animated filmmaking.” Some of that effort came at human cost: animators publicly complained about working conditions during production, and the issue contributed to the long delay of the third film.

Reason 2: The Script Refuses Easy Sequel Logic

Phil Lord and Chris Miller (co-writers with David Callaham) made a structural choice few sequel scripts attempt: Across the Spider-Verse is functionally Part 1 of 2. The film does not resolve. It ends on a literal cliffhanger with Miles trapped in the wrong dimension and Gwen mounting a rescue. That kind of ending normally gets punished by audiences (see: Mockingjay Part 1, Fantastic Beasts sequels), and yet here it lands.

The reason is that the script earns it. The film's central thematic argument — that the Spider-Society's belief in “canon events” (the recurring tragedies that supposedly must happen to every Spider-character) is a self-justifying lie used to protect institutional comfort — gets fully argued, dramatized, and concluded by the cliffhanger. The plot is unresolved. The theme is not.

Reason 3: Miles Morales Is the Best-Written Superhero of the Decade

Shameik Moore's vocal performance and the writing for Miles Morales add up to something unusual in superhero cinema: a young protagonist whose moral compass is the most reliable thing in his universe, surrounded by adult characters whose certainty about “how things have to be” is steadily revealed to be cowardice. Most coming-of-age superhero arcs put the hero on a journey from naive to responsible. Miles starts responsible. The film puts him on the inverse journey: from trusting institutions to dismantling them.

The lobby scene with Miguel O'Hara — where Miles is told that his father is fated to die and that any attempt to save him will collapse Miles's entire universe — is the film's emotional centerpiece. Miles's response is to run. The film's argument is that running is the correct moral choice.

Reason 4: The Soundtrack Did the Heavy Lifting

Daniel Pemberton's score is one of the most-praised film scores of the decade. The Gwen Stacy theme alone has crossed 50 million Spotify streams as a standalone track. The film also features 26 needle-drops across genres, including a soundtrack album executive-produced by Metro Boomin that hit #2 on the Billboard 200.

The music is doing structural work, not just decoration. The Mumbattan sequence's transition from western-style scoring to Indian classical instrumentation is doing the cross-cultural argument the visuals alone could not. The Spot's villain motif uses dissonance to telegraph that he is going to matter more than he appears in his first scene. These are not accidents.

Reason 5: It Treats Its Audience as Adults

This is the rarest thing in modern animated filmmaking and probably the biggest single reason for the rating. The film does not condescend. It does not explain its themes in dialogue. It expects you to follow a multiversal plot with six distinct visual languages, track twelve characters, and accept a 140-minute runtime with a cliffhanger ending. That trust pays off: the audiences that show up for it leave more invested than they entered.
