Wuthering Heights (2026) Review: Margot Robbie & Jacob Elordi’s Stormy HBO Max Hit

Updated: May 8, 2026 11 min read

TL;DR

Emerald Fennell’s Wuthering Heights reimagining stars Margot Robbie as Catherine and Jacob Elordi as Heathcliff. Critics are split (57% on Rotten Tomatoes), audiences leaned warmer (76%), and the IMDb rating sits at 6.2. It is rare for a 19th-century literary adaptation to dominate streaming charts in 2026, but Wuthering Heights is not a typical adaptation. Emerald Fennell — the writer-director behind Promising Young Woman and Saltburn — brought her trademark visual maximalism, tonal whiplash, and willingness to provoke to Emily Brontë’s gothic romance. The result is the most argued-about prestige film of the year, and it has now become an HBO Max phenomenon.

What the Movie Actually Is

Fennell’s adaptation keeps the bones of Brontë’s 1847 novel — the moors, the foundling Heathcliff, the obsessive love between him and Catherine Earnshaw, the generational damage that echoes outward — and then reframes the whole thing as a heightened psychodrama. There is more sex, more blood, more saturated color, more pop-music-needle-drop than the source material would suggest. There is also more humor, much of it pitch-black.

This is not a faithful Masterpiece Theatre version of Wuthering Heights. It is closer in tone to Saltburn with petticoats. Whether that sounds thrilling or appalling depends entirely on what you wanted out of the film going in.

Margot Robbie as Catherine

Robbie was the casting decision that made the movie an event. At 35 (with Robbie a bit older than the typical Catherine) she leans into Cathy’s self-destructive theatricality rather than her teenage volatility. Reviews of her performance have been broadly positive even from critics who disliked the film, with several outlets singling out her ability to make Cathy’s contradictions land — the cruelty laced with longing, the manipulation of the men around her, the awareness of her own performance.

This is one of those roles that may grow in reputation faster than the surrounding film. Even viewers who walked out frustrated tend to concede that Robbie understood the assignment.

Jacob Elordi as Heathcliff

Elordi has the harder job. Heathcliff is one of literature’s most contested characters — brooding, abused, abusive, charismatic, monstrous, depending on which paragraph you stop on. Casting him at all is a controversy magnet, and Fennell’s adaptation made it more so. Critics were divided on whether Elordi adds new shadings to the character or simply borrows the Saltburn vibe of his last collaboration with the director.

Audiences, judging by social-media response, were significantly more enthusiastic. The Cathy/Heathcliff chemistry is real, and the central scenes work whether or not you buy the larger movie around them.

Why Critics and Audiences Diverged

A 19-point gap between the Rotten Tomatoes Tomatometer (57%) and audience score (76%) is large — enough to flag the film as a critic/audience divider. A few likely reasons:

  • Genre expectation mismatch. Critics graded it as a literary adaptation; many viewers came in expecting a stylish romantic drama and got more of that than expected.
  • Fennell fatigue or fandom. Critics who already had strong feelings about Saltburn often carried them forward. Fans of Saltburn were primed to enjoy this; Saltburn skeptics doubled down.
  • The R-rated content. The film’s explicit scenes were dealbreakers for some critics and a feature for some audiences. Tone is rarely scored in a vacuum.
  • The Brontë question. Some critics thought Fennell’s reframing flattened the novel’s racial subtext rather than engaging with it. Audiences less familiar with the book were less likely to flag this.

How It Compares to Previous Wuthering Heights Films

Wuthering Heights has been adapted to screen at least a dozen times, including the 1939 William Wyler classic with Laurence Olivier, the 1992 Ralph Fiennes/Juliette Binoche version, and Andrea Arnold’s gritty 2011 take. Fennell’s 2026 version is the most stylized, the most explicit, and probably the most popular at scale — but it is also the most divisive among critics.

If you are choosing where to start, the 1939 film remains the consensus pick for tradition (it earned an Oscar nomination for Best Picture). Andrea Arnold’s 2011 version is the favorite of the literary-fidelity camp, despite its small theatrical footprint. Fennell’s is the one to watch for what big-studio prestige looks like in 2026.

Should You Watch It?

Watch it if:

  • You loved Saltburn or even just respected its commitment to going there
  • You are a Margot Robbie completist (this is one of her riskier swings)
  • You enjoy stylized, tonally extreme, R-rated period dramas
  • You want to be in on the year’s most-discussed prestige film

Skip it if:

  • You wanted a faithful, restrained literary adaptation
  • You did not enjoy Saltburn’s tonal extremism
  • Explicit content in period drama settings is a hard line for you

The Bottom Line

Wuthering Heights (2026) is the kind of film that resists a single rating. It is too uneven for unconditional praise and too memorable for outright dismissal. The 6.2 IMDb captures the polarization rather than a quality verdict. If your taste runs toward stylized prestige cinema with sharp edges, this is a strong recommend. If you wanted Brontë on its own terms, it will frustrate you. Either way, after $242 million at the box office and HBO Max’s #1 spot in 32 countries, this is the cultural conversation of May 2026.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the IMDb rating for Wuthering Heights (2026)?

Wuthering Heights (2026) currently holds an IMDb rating of 6.2 out of 10. The Rotten Tomatoes Tomatometer is 57% with critics; the audience score is a notably higher 76%.

Where can I stream Wuthering Heights (2026)?

It began streaming on HBO Max on May 1, 2026, after a successful theatrical run that earned roughly $242 million worldwide. Within 24 hours of streaming release, it was the #1 film on HBO Max in 32 countries.

Who directed Wuthering Heights (2026)?

Emerald Fennell, the filmmaker behind Promising Young Woman (Best Original Screenplay Oscar) and Saltburn. Her stylized, provocative voice is consistent across all three films.

Is Wuthering Heights (2026) faithful to the book?

It takes significant liberties. The central Cathy and Heathcliff arc is intact, but Fennell reframes their relationship as a heightened, R-rated psychodrama with modern stylistic flourishes. Purists may struggle; Saltburn fans will likely feel at home.
